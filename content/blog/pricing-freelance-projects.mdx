For the first couple of years of freelancing, I billed hourly. It felt fair — you pay for the time I spend — but it quietly worked against both sides. Clients worried every hour was a meter running, and I was penalized for getting faster at my job.

## Price the outcome, not the hours

Once I moved to fixed-price milestones tied to concrete deliverables — "user authentication and onboarding flow," not "40 hours of backend work" — conversations got easier. Clients could compare a price to the value of the feature, not to some abstract hourly rate.

## Scope tightly, then quote confidently

Fixed pricing only works if the scope is genuinely fixed. That means a real discovery phase before any number gets attached: what screens exist, what the API needs to do, which edge cases matter. Vague scope is the actual risk in fixed pricing, not the pricing model itself.

## Build in a change-request process from day one

Scope creep isn't a client problem, it's a process gap. Every proposal I send now includes an explicit "anything outside this list is a change request, quoted separately" clause. It's rarely invoked, but having it in writing keeps both sides aligned when a "quick addition" comes up mid-project.

## Charge a discovery fee for larger projects

For anything beyond a small MVP, I now charge a small, separate fee for the discovery and scoping phase itself. It filters for clients who are serious, and it means the scoping work — which is real, valuable work — isn't given away for free before a contract exists.

None of this made pricing perfectly simple. It made it something clients and I could actually have a straightforward conversation about.
